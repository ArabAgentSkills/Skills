# Objection Handling

Classify the objection before answering. Do not overcome objections with invented facts or pressure.

## Objection Types

- Need: buyer does not see the pain.
- Fit: buyer doubts sector, country, workflow, or language fit.
- Trust: buyer doubts credibility, references, local support, or delivery.
- Price: buyer compares cost, ROI, or budget.
- Procurement: buyer needs portal, tender, vendor registration, or approved partner status.
- Security/legal: buyer needs compliance, data residency, DPA, privacy, or security review.
- Timing: buyer has no current budget cycle or active initiative.
- Partner conflict: buyer already has an incumbent provider or channel partner.

## Response Pattern

1. Acknowledge the objection.
2. Ask one clarifying question.
3. Provide specific proof or admit the unknown.
4. Offer a low-risk next step.
5. Flag review if the answer touches law, regulation, procurement, data, money, health, identity, telecom, tax, payroll, or public sector.

## Examples

Procurement objection:

```text
That makes sense. Before we suggest a next step, can you confirm whether evaluation must happen through a supplier portal, tender, approved vendor list, or a local partner? We can prepare a short capability note for review, but any procurement route should be confirmed by your process owner.
```

Trust objection:

```text
Fair concern. I do not want to overclaim local proof. The safest next step is a focused pilot scope with success criteria, data boundaries, and a named support owner. If local references are required, we should only use references that are approved for this buyer and country.
```

## Do Not Invent

- Local customer references.
- Government approvals.
- Bank, telco, or payment partnerships.
- Guaranteed procurement shortcuts.
- Compliance certifications.
- Discount authority.
- Integration coverage.
